In October 2020, the South Korean government announced their 2050 Carbon Neutrality scenario, which includes reducing GHG emissions by 40% compared to 2018 by the end of 2030. Achieving this is a highly challenging task since it requires a reduction in GHG emissions from 728 million tons in 2018 to 436.6 million tons within the next eight years.

However, despite this challenging goal, the South Korean government is still maintaining its stance on phasing out from nuclear power plants.

Why is Nuclear Energy so crucial in achieving Carbon Neutrality?

Nuclear Power is one of the most environmentally friendly energy sources available.

Nuclear power produces energy via nuclear fission of Uranium, generating base-load electricity with no carbon output. The only GHG emissions from nuclear power generation come from the ancillary use of fossil fuels during processes including construction, fuel processing, and maintenance of the nuclear power plants. This GHG emission is still a relatively small amount compared to low-carbon/renewable energy sources.

Nuclear Power is highly effective and reliable compared to other energy sources.

Nuclear power has a high capacity factor of approximately 93%, which means it operates at its maximum capacity of 93% of the time it's operating. Nuclear power is almost twice as reliable as current carbon-based energy plants of coal and gas, and 2.5 - 3.5 times more reliable than renewable energy plants. Nuclear power is highly effective and reliable compared to other renewable energy sources.

Nuclear Power is already producing more energy than any renewable energy sources.

Nuclear power has the highest share in total energy production among low-carbon energy sources. Since the first operation of a nuclear power plant in 1986, energy production from nuclear power plants has been constantly increasing, taking up approximately 12% of the total energy production in South Korea. Given the highly challenging aims of South Korea's carbon neutrality plan and the fact that nuclear power is already producing more energy than any other low-carbon-based energy source, it is reasonable to conclude that we should be utilizing more nuclear power for the net-zero plan rather than phasing out from it.

Unlike the common misconception, Nuclear Power is one of the most safest energy source.

Nuclear power is one of the safest energy sources, despite common misconceptions. It has saved millions of lives by replacing energy production from fossil fuels. Even including catastrophic incidents such as Chernobyl and Fukushima, data shows that nuclear power is very close to the safest form of energy production out of all sources. The death rate from energy production per TWh is 0.07, which is almost the same as renewable energy sources such as wind or solar.

Moreover, nuclear power releases less radiation into the environment than any other major energy source. For example, coal produces a material called fly ash when it's burnt, releasing nearly 100 times more radiation into the environment than a nuclear power plant for the same amount of energy production.

Evaluating Low-carbon Energy sources

Nuclear power is clearly an advantageous energy source compared to other low-carbon/renewable energy sources. Each energy source was given points out of 5 on each criteria, by standardising the corresponding data of all energy sources and converting it into a scale of 1 - 5. From the chart, nuclear power covers relatively high points all-around in 5 criteria.

So, what's the conclusion?

Nuclear power isn't the only solution to achieve the carbon neutrality and it's definitely has it's downside being a non-renewable source of energy. However to achieve carbon neutrality by 2050, it is inevitable to utilise nuclear energy until the technology for renewable energy production is further developed and commercialised. Whilst there are yet to be solved issues regarding nuclear energy, such as waste disposal, nuclear energy deserves a lot better than the anti-nuclear prejudices and fears that have plagued in. It's valuable, irreplaceable, and a critical part of solution to the greatest energy threat in the history of humankind.
